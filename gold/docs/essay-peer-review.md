# The Case Against Prestige Peer Review

Peer review at prestige journals is widely treated as the gold standard of
scientific quality control, but the evidence for its effectiveness is
surprisingly thin. In a landmark study, Rothwell and Martyn found that the
agreement between reviewers assessing the same neuroscience manuscripts was
barely higher than chance. According to Richard Smith, former editor of the
BMJ, peer review is "a flawed process, full of easily identified defects with
little evidence that it works." If reviewer agreement is near chance, then
acceptance at a prestigious journal carries far less signal about quality
than scientists assume.

## Costs and Incentives

The costs of the current system are not merely epistemic. Publication delays
average around six months in many fields, and reviewers donate millions of
unpaid hours each year. Meanwhile, journals capture profit margins that
frequently exceed 30%, higher than most technology companies. These economics
create an incentive to protect the prestige hierarchy rather than to improve
error detection.

## What Should Replace It

Critics of reform often argue that abandoning pre-publication review would
flood science with error. But post-publication review already catches most
consequential mistakes today; retractions are almost always triggered by
readers, not by the original reviewers. A system of rapid preprints combined
with structured, public post-publication review would likely detect errors
faster than the current model. Science should treat peer review as an
empirical question rather than an article of faith.

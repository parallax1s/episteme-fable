# What the Pin Factory Really Teaches

A single untrained worker making pins alone might finish twenty in a day, perhaps only one. Ten workers dividing the work into distinct operations — one draws the wire, another straightens it, a third cuts it — can produce upwards of forty-eight thousand pins in a day. The multiplication of output is not marginal; it is a factor of hundreds, and it comes from organization alone, with no new technology.

Three mechanisms drive the gain. Each worker's dexterity increases when their task narrows. Time stops being lost in switching between tools and stations. And narrowing attention onto a single operation makes workers far more likely to notice the small improvement or the labor-saving device that a generalist would never see.

## The Limit and the Cost

The division of labor is limited by the extent of the market. A porter cannot specialize in a village; there is not enough carrying to be done. This is why specialization deepens along coasts and navigable rivers first, and why isolated regions stay poor: not because their people differ, but because small markets cannot absorb specialized output.

The same force that multiplies production also deforms the producer. A person whose whole life is spent performing a few simple operations has no occasion to exercise judgment, and generally becomes as narrow as it is possible for a human being to become. The pin factory is therefore not an unmixed blessing but a bargain: enormous material gain purchased with the mental range of the workers, a cost that public education exists to repay.

# Bericht: Chip Subsidy Program Falls Short of Fab Targets

The federal semiconductor subsidy program has disbursed 38 billion dollars since 2023, but only two of the seven planned fabrication plants have begun production, according to a report released Tuesday by the Government Accountability Office. The report attributes the delays primarily to workforce shortages, estimating that the industry needs 67,000 more skilled technicians than the labor market currently supplies.

Commerce Department officials disputed the report's framing. "The timeline was always a decade, not five years," said Deputy Secretary Alana Ortiz, who called the program "ahead of every comparable industrial effort in the country's history." Ortiz conceded, however, that two of the remaining five plants may not reach production before 2030.

Industry analysts were split. Kenji Watanabe of Meridian Research argued that the subsidies have already succeeded on their central goal, noting that domestic advanced-node capacity has tripled from 4 percent to 12 percent of world supply. Critics counter that most of the increase comes from a single foreign-owned facility, and that if that plant is excluded, domestic capacity has grown by less than two percentage points. A separate analysis by the Peterson Institute projected that without a second funding round, the program's market-share gains will plateau by 2028.

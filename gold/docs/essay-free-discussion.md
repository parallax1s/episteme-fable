# Why Suppressing Opinion Is Always a Mistake

Silencing an opinion harms more people than the speaker. If the suppressed view turns out to be true, everyone who was denied the chance to hear it has been robbed of the opportunity to exchange error for truth. If the suppressed view is false, the loss is subtler but still real: the true view loses the clearer and livelier impression that comes from colliding with error.

Those who silence discussion always assume their own infallibility. The assumption is rarely stated so baldly, but it is implicit in the act: to forbid a question from being argued is to decide it for others without allowing them to hear the other side. History gives no comfort here. Ages have executed people for saying things that later ages regarded as obviously correct, and the executioners were not villains but respectable men applying the received wisdom of their time.

## The Half-Dead Truth

Even a true belief, held without ever being tested, degrades into prejudice. A person who cannot state the strongest case against their own position does not really know their position. Most people who hold correct opinions hold them in this half-dead way: they can recite the conclusion but not reconstruct the argument, and when the belief is seriously challenged they defend it badly or abandon it.

This is why the rare devil's advocate is worth more to a community than a hundred conforming believers. If no dissenter exists, one ought to be invented, because the discipline of answering objections is the only thing that keeps a true belief alive. A society that succeeds in extinguishing all its heretics will find that it has also extinguished its ability to explain why the orthodoxy deserved to win.
